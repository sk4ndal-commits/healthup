# Guidelines

## 1. Product intent

This app exists to help busy professionals stay consistent with their health **when real life gets messy**.

It is designed for people who already know the basics of healthy behavior, but repeatedly lose momentum when work stress, fragmented schedules, low energy, travel, or routine disruption hit.

The product goal is not to create perfect users.  
The product goal is to help users maintain **durable health consistency** under normal modern pressure.

The core promise is:

> Build a realistic health routine that adapts to real life and helps users stay on track without turning health into another job.

---

## 2. What the app is

This app is:

- a **daily health execution tool**
- a **consistency system**, not just a tracker
- a **decision-reduction product** that tells users what matters today
- an **adaptive routine app** that scales down on hard days instead of collapsing
- a **weekly rebalancing system** that adjusts plans based on actual behavior
- a **consumer product** built for normal users, not elite optimizers

At its core, the app translates health into a small number of repeatable actions across:

- sleep
- movement
- exercise
- nutrition
- recovery

The app should help users do three things especially well:

1. know what matters today
2. preserve momentum on hard days
3. reset intelligently each week

---

## 3. What the app is not

This app is **not**:

- a generic wellness content app
- a social fitness network
- a calorie-counting-first product
- a bodybuilding or performance-optimization platform
- a medical device
- a therapy replacement
- a habit tracker with 20 equal-priority tasks
- a guilt machine driven by streak pressure
- a quantified-self dashboard for biohackers
- an app that assumes every day should be an ideal day

It should not become:

- over-analytical
- too abstract
- too educational
- too dependent on manual logging
- too complex to use when the user is tired

If a feature increases cognitive load more than it increases useful action, it is probably wrong for this product.

---

## 4. User problem this app solves

The target user does not primarily suffer from lack of knowledge.

They suffer from:

- inconsistency
- overambitious planning
- routine collapse during stressful weeks
- all-or-nothing thinking
- decision fatigue
- repeated restarts
- loss of self-trust

The real problem is:

> "I care about my health, but I cannot maintain my routine when life gets intense."

The app exists to solve exactly that.

---

## 5. Primary user

### Core ICP
Busy knowledge workers, roughly 28–45, who care about their health, have tried building routines before, but repeatedly fall off during stressful or fragmented weeks.

### User characteristics
They usually:

- have demanding jobs and moderate to high cognitive load
- are not beginners, but are not advanced athletes
- want more energy, better fitness, and more routine stability
- dislike guilt-heavy fitness culture
- want structure without complexity
- are willing to pay for something that reduces mental load and improves consistency

### Important user truth
They do not need more health advice.  
They need a system that helps them stay consistent under pressure.

---

## 6. Core product philosophy

These principles should guide product decisions, even if users never see the internal language.

### 6.1 Consistency over intensity
Repeated workable behavior matters more than heroic effort.

### 6.2 Flexible success
The app must treat reduced plans, fallback days, and minimum wins as valid progress.

### 6.3 Adaptation over rigidity
The plan must respond to energy, stress, and real constraints.

### 6.4 Decision reduction over feature expansion
The product should reduce user thinking load, not increase it.

### 6.5 Rebalancing over self-blame
When a plan fails, the system should adapt before the user disengages.

### 6.6 Capacity over aesthetics alone
The product should frame health as energy, resilience, and sustainable functioning, not just appearance.

### 6.7 Calm credibility over hype
The product should feel serious, practical, modern, and trustworthy.

---

## 7. Core product loop

The MVP loop is:

1. onboarding creates a realistic baseline plan
2. the Today screen shows what matters now
3. the user logs Good / Better / Best completion
4. the app adapts on low-energy or high-stress days
5. the weekly review identifies the bottleneck
6. the next week is adjusted accordingly

This loop must stay intact.

If future features weaken this loop, they should be questioned.

---

## 8. Main UX principles

### 8.1 The app must feel lightweight
The user should be able to open the app and understand what to do in seconds.

### 8.2 The app must feel emotionally safe
No shame-based copy. No failure framing. No “you are behind” energy.

### 8.3 The app must reward partial completion
The product must visibly value the minimum viable win.

### 8.4 The app must be usable on depleted days
Design for tired, stressed, overloaded people—not idealized users.

### 8.5 The app must prioritize clarity
At any point, the user should know:

- what matters today
- what still counts
- what changed
- what to do next

### 8.6 The app must avoid noise
Do not surface too many metrics, decisions, choices, or explanations at once.

---

## 9. Product tone and brand behavior

The product voice should be:

- calm
- supportive
- credible
- direct
- practical
- non-judgmental
- quietly motivating

It should not sound:

- preachy
- childish
- extreme
- clinical
- overly spiritual
- hyper-masculine or hyper-performative
- like a productivity app cosplaying as health

### Good language examples
- Stay on track
- Protect momentum
- Focus on what matters today
- Keep the basics alive
- Adjust the plan
- A lighter version still counts
- Build consistency

### Avoid language like
- crush it
- no excuses
- optimize everything
- perfect streak
- elite
- unlock your peak self
- fail / failed / failure-oriented copy

---

## 10. Feature guardrails

A new feature should only be added if it clearly strengthens at least one of these:

- daily clarity
- consistency
- fallback execution
- weekly rebalancing
- visible progress without overload
- trust in the system

### Strong candidate features
- simpler daily planning
- better fallback suggestions
- improved weekly review
- easier plan editing
- low-friction progress visibility
- lightweight integrations that reduce user effort

### Risky features
- social feeds
- complex challenge systems
- excessive gamification
- advanced analytics dashboards
- deep calorie tracking
- too many integrations too early
- educational content libraries
- AI features that add talk without adding action

### Rule of thumb
If a feature mainly increases engagement vanity metrics but does not improve consistency, it is likely misaligned.

---

## 11. Success definition

The product is successful if users become more consistent over time.

### North-star outcome
Users complete at least the minimum viable version of their plan on more days, across more weeks, with fewer full resets.

### Strong product signals
- users stay active during stressful weeks
- users use fallback modes instead of disengaging
- users report lower all-or-nothing thinking
- users trust the plan more
- users feel less guilt and more control
- users maintain routine continuity longer than before

### Weak or misleading signals
- app opens without behavior change
- high logging volume with low retention
- heavy feature usage that does not improve consistency
- time spent in app as a proxy for value

The goal is not more screen time.  
The goal is more durable health behavior.

---

## 12. MVP scope doctrine

### Must-have in MVP
- lightweight onboarding
- realistic first plan
- Today screen
- Good / Better / Best completion
- Busy / Recovery modes
- daily quick check-in
- weekly review
- bottleneck detection
- simple progress view
- editable core routine settings

### Must not be required in MVP
- wearables
- social features
- full calorie tracking
- coaching marketplace
- biomarker integrations
- rich journaling
- community
- clinical health advice
- complex AI coach behavior

The MVP should be tight, opinionated, and operational.

## 14. Design principles for implementation

### 14.1 One main thing per screen
Do not ask the user to process multiple priorities at once.

### 14.2 Primary CTA must always be obvious
The user should immediately know the main action.

### 14.3 Fast interactions win
Prefer taps over forms. Prefer defaults over setup burden.

### 14.4 The app should look calm
Use visual hierarchy, whitespace, and restraint.

### 14.5 Progress must feel human
Show trends and momentum, not judgment.

### 14.6 No feature should make the user feel “behind”
This is a critical guardrail.

---

## 15. Decision rules for product teams

When debating any feature or UX choice, ask:

1. Does this make daily action clearer?
2. Does this reduce or increase mental effort?
3. Does this help on the user’s worst day, not just their best day?
4. Does this preserve momentum when the ideal plan breaks?
5. Does this support consistency rather than perfection?
6. Does this make the weekly reset more useful?
7. Would an average tired user understand this instantly?

If the answer to several of these is no, the idea likely does not belong in the product.

---

## 16. Technical product framing

### Mobile app
The main product is the Flutter mobile app. It must be optimized for fast daily use, local-first behavior, and low-friction interactions.

### Backend
ASP.NET Web API with Postgres supports persistence, future sync, admin visibility, and product iteration.

### Admin web app
React web app is internal-only. It exists to inspect users, plans, reviews, and plan generation behavior—not to serve end users.

---

## 17. Final product statement

This product is a **real-life health consistency system** for busy professionals.

It helps users:

- know what matters today
- keep going on hard days
- adapt instead of collapse
- build trust in a routine that actually fits real life

That is the standard the product should hold.

Any feature, copy, design choice, or metric that weakens that clarity should be reconsidered.
